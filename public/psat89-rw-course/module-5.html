This set introduces four advanced patterns. Tap each card to reveal what makes them tricky and how to handle them.

Quantitative Evidence
Read data carefully ‚Äî wrong answers misstate, reverse, or exaggerate numbers. Ask: "Does the answer match what the data actually says?"
üí° Watch for: "Identical" when numbers are close but not equal. "Always" or "proves" from a single study. Reversed comparisons (saying A > B when data shows B > A). Overgeneralization from one task to "all tasks."
‚ñº
Two-Part Wrong Answers
At higher difficulty, wrong answers get one thing right and one wrong. Read every word of every choice.
üí° Strategy: Break each answer choice into its individual claims. Check each claim against the passage separately. If ANY part is wrong, the whole answer is wrong ‚Äî even if the other part is perfect.
‚ñº
Tone and Attitude
Look for loaded words, sarcasm, and the gap between what's described and HOW it's described.
üí° Strategy: Circle adjectives and descriptive phrases ‚Äî they reveal attitude. Sarcasm = saying the opposite of what you mean. If the author calls something a "masterclass" but clearly thinks it's terrible, that's sardonic. Neutral tone uses no evaluative language.
‚ñº
Semicolons + Conjunctive Adverbs
Pattern: [clause]; however, / moreover, / therefore, [clause]. Semicolon before, comma after.
üí° Rule: Conjunctive adverbs (however, moreover, therefore, nevertheless, consequently) are NOT conjunctions. They can't join clauses with just a comma. Use: semicolon + adverb + comma, OR period + Adverb + comma.

Summary

Key Takeaways

  1. Quantitative evidence questions test whether you read data precisely ‚Äî "close" is not "identical," and one study doesn't "prove" anything.
  2. At advanced difficulty, wrong answers are designed to be half-right. Break each choice into separate claims and verify each one.
  3. Tone questions require you to distinguish WHAT is described from HOW the author describes it. Loaded words and irony are key signals.
  4. Words-in-context at this level test subtle secondary meanings. The most obvious definition is almost always the trap.
  5. A score drop on harder material is progress ‚Äî you're building the skills to handle test-day surprises.
