Unit Conversion & Dimensional Analysis
‚ñ∂

Multiply by conversion factors so unwanted units cancel:

Example: Convert 60 mph to feet per second:

60 miles/hr √ó 5280 ft/mile √ó 1 hr/3600 sec = 88 ft/sec

The trick: set up fractions so miles cancel with miles, hours cancel with hours, leaving you with the units you want.

SAT tip: Watch for unit conversion traps ‚Äî the question gives you data in minutes but asks for hours, or gives miles but asks for kilometers.

Direct vs. Inverse Proportion
‚ñ∂

Direct proportion: y = kx. As x increases, y increases. Doubling x doubles y. Example: distance = rate √ó time (at constant rate).

Inverse proportion: y = k/x. As x increases, y decreases. Doubling x halves y. Example: if 6 workers take 10 hours, 12 workers take 5 hours.

To identify: if the product xy is constant ‚Üí inverse. If the ratio y/x is constant ‚Üí direct.

Topic 5B

Statistics: Center, Spread & Shape

Mean vs. Median ‚Äî When It Matters
‚ñ∂

Mean = sum of values √∑ number of values. Sensitive to outliers.

Median = middle value (when sorted). Resistant to outliers.

The SAT's favorite question: "A very high value is added to the data set. Which measure of center is more affected?"

Answer: The mean is always more affected by outliers. The median barely budges.

Example: Data: 10, 12, 14, 15, 16. Mean = 13.4, Median = 14.

Add an outlier (100): 10, 12, 14, 15, 16, 100. Mean = 27.8 (jumped!), Median = 14.5 (barely changed).

Standard Deviation & Spread
‚ñ∂

Range = max ‚àí min. Simplest measure of spread.

Standard deviation = how spread out values are from the mean. You don't need to calculate it ‚Äî just understand the concept:

‚Ä¢ Data clustered tightly around the mean ‚Üí SMALL standard deviation

‚Ä¢ Data spread far from the mean ‚Üí LARGE standard deviation

‚Ä¢ Adding/subtracting a constant to every value does NOT change the standard deviation

‚Ä¢ Multiplying every value by a constant DOES change the standard deviation (by that factor)

Distribution Shape
‚ñ∂

Symmetric: Mean ‚âà Median. Bell-shaped or uniform.

Skewed right (positive skew): Tail extends right. Mean > Median. (A few very high values pull the mean up.)

Skewed left (negative skew): Tail extends left. Mean < Median. (A few very low values pull the mean down.)

SAT tip: "In which direction is the distribution skewed?" ‚Üí look for the long tail.

Topic 5C

Data Interpretation

Scatterplots & Line of Best Fit
‚ñ∂

Correlation: Positive (both increase), Negative (one up, other down), None (no pattern).

Line of best fit: Slope = rate of change in context. Y-intercept = starting/initial value.

Interpolation = predicting WITHIN the data range (more reliable).

Extrapolation = predicting BEYOND the data range (less reliable).

Residuals = actual ‚àí predicted. A good model has residuals randomly scattered around zero. If residuals show a pattern (like a curve), the model is a bad fit.

Two-Way Frequency Tables
‚ñ∂

Two-way tables organize data by two categorical variables. Key skills:

Marginal frequencies: The row/column totals.

Joint frequencies: The values inside the table.

Conditional probability: P(A|B) = "given B, what's the probability of A?"

Formula: P(A|B) = (count of A AND B) / (total count of B)

Example: 40 students: 15 play sports AND have a job. 25 total play sports. P(has job | plays sports) = 15/25 = 60%.

Desmos Regression: Enter data as a table, then type y‚ÇÅ ~ mx‚ÇÅ + b for linear or y‚ÇÅ ~ ax‚ÇÅ¬≤ + bx‚ÇÅ + c for quadratic. Desmos finds the best-fit equation automatically.

Topic 5D

Probability & Study Design

Probability Basics
‚ñ∂

P(event) = favorable outcomes / total outcomes

Probability is always between 0 (impossible) and 1 (certain).

Complement: P(not A) = 1 ‚àí P(A)

"At least one" problems: P(at least one) = 1 ‚àí P(none)

Study Design ‚Äî The SAT's Favorite Trick
‚ñ∂

The SAT tests whether you understand what conclusions are valid from a study. Three key rules:

1. Random sampling ‚Üí results can be generalized to the population.

If the sample is NOT random (e.g., only volunteers), you can NOT generalize.

2. Random assignment to groups ‚Üí causation can be established.

This is what makes it an EXPERIMENT vs. an observational study.

3. No random assignment ‚Üí only association/correlation, NOT causation.

Confounding variables: Other factors that could explain the result. "Students who eat breakfast score higher" ‚Äî is it breakfast, or are breakfast-eaters also more organized?

Margin of error: Larger sample ‚Üí smaller margin of error. That's it. That's the whole concept.

Key Takeaways

  1. Percent change = (new ‚àí old) / old √ó 100. The OLD value is always the denominator.
