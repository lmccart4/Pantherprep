Rhetorical Purpose Questions
‚ñº

These questions ask why the author made a specific choice. Common phrasings: "The author most likely includes the example of X in order to‚Ä¶" or "The primary function of the third sentence is to‚Ä¶"

The 4-Step Purpose Analysis

  1. Locate the specific element the question asks about.
  2. Look at what comes before and after ‚Äî purpose is always about context. What claim or point does this element serve?
  3. Ask: What would be LOST if this element were removed? That tells you its purpose.
  4. Match your analysis to the answer choices. Beware of answers that describe what the element says rather than what it does.
Key Distinction: "What it says" ‚â† "What it does." A statistic might say "37% of coral reefs have bleached" but it does "provide quantitative evidence supporting the claim that coral degradation is widespread."

Text Structure Questions
‚ñº

These questions ask how a passage is organized or how one part relates to the whole.

The 6 Common SAT Structures

Claim ‚Üí Evidence
Author states a position, then provides supporting data, examples, or expert testimony.
Problem ‚Üí Solution
Presents a challenge, then describes how it was (or could be) addressed.
Cause ‚Üí Effect
Describes an event or condition and traces its consequences.
Compare / Contrast
Examines similarities and/or differences between two or more things.
Chronological / Narrative
Presents events or developments in time order.
Concession ‚Üí Rebuttal
Acknowledges a counterpoint ("While X is true‚Ä¶") then argues against it ("however, Y‚Ä¶").

‚ñº

These questions ask you to characterize the author's attitude or explain why a particular word was chosen. The key is having precise vocabulary for describing tone.

The Tone Spectrum

Most SAT tone answers fall on a spectrum from negative to positive. The trick is choosing the answer with the right intensity ‚Äî the SAT almost always prefers moderate tones.

Synthesis & Dual-Passage Questions
‚ñº

Some SAT questions present two related texts or data + text and ask you to connect them.

Strategy for Paired Texts

  1. Read Text 1 completely. Identify its main claim.
  2. Read Text 2 completely. Identify its main claim.
  3. Before looking at answers, ask: How do these two claims relate?

Common Relationships

Agreement: Text 2 supports or extends Text 1's argument.
Disagreement: Text 2 challenges or contradicts Text 1.
Qualification: Text 2 partially agrees but adds limits or conditions.
Different Scope: Same topic, different aspects or perspectives.

Summary

Key Takeaways

  1. "What it says" ‚â† "What it does." Rhetorical purpose is about function, not content.
  2. For purpose questions: look at what comes before and after, and ask what would be lost without it.
  3. Know the 6 common text structures ‚Äî they appear repeatedly on the SAT.
  4. Tone answers require precise vocabulary. Match both direction AND intensity.
  5. For paired texts: identify each text's claim FIRST, then determine the relationship.
  6. The SAT rewards moderate, nuanced answers. Extreme tones are almost always wrong.
