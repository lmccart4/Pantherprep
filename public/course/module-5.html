Core Framework

The Three Key Questions

Core Principle: These three questions should become automatic ‚Äî ask them silently as you read every single SAT passage.
1
BIG IDEA
What is the main point the author wants you to understand? Usually in the first or last sentence.
2
STRUCTURE
How is the passage organized? Cause-effect? Comparison? Claim-evidence? Problem-solution?
3
TONE
What is the author's attitude? Neutral? Enthusiastic? Skeptical? Critical? Tone eliminates wrong answers.
Question Types & Strategies

How to Attack Each Question Type

Main Idea Questions
‚ñº

Main idea questions ask you to identify the central point or best summary of the passage. They are among the most common question types.

The "Predict and Match" Technique

  1. Read the passage completely (it's short ‚Äî don't skip to the question).
  2. Before looking at answers, mentally answer Key Question 1: What is the big idea?
  3. Form your own answer in your head FIRST.
  4. Now read the choices. Eliminate any that are:

üîçToo Narrow

Only covers one detail from the passage, not the full picture.

üåäToo Broad

Goes beyond what the passage actually discusses.

üîÑDistorted

Takes a real detail but changes its meaning or significance.

‚õîContradicted

Directly opposes something stated in the passage.

The correct answer will match the scope and focus of the full passage ‚Äî not too specific, not too general.

üìå
Evidence-Based Questions
‚ñº

These questions ask you to identify which part of a text supports a particular claim.

Cardinal Rule: You must be able to point to specific words in the passage that support your answer. If you can't point to them, eliminate the choice.

Strategy

  1. Read the claim carefully. What exactly does it say?
  2. For each evidence choice, ask: "Does this DIRECTLY prove the claim, or does it just relate to the same topic?"
  3. The evidence must be specific and direct ‚Äî not vaguely related.
  4. "Relates to the topic" ‚â† "supports the claim." This is the trap.
‚úì Direct evidence
Claim: Musical training improves academic performance.
Evidence: "Children who began piano at age six scored 15% higher on math tests."
‚Üí Directly proves the claim with specific data.
‚úó Topic-related but not evidence
Same claim.
Evidence: "Music programs have been cut in many school districts."
‚Üí Same topic (music), but says nothing about academic performance.

Inference Questions
‚ñº

Inference questions ask what can be reasonably concluded based on the passage. The key word is "reasonably." SAT inferences are small, safe logical steps ‚Äî never wild leaps.

The Golden Rule of SAT Inferences: SAT inferences are BORING. They are the least exciting, most directly supported conclusion. If an answer feels like an interesting insight that goes beyond the text, it's almost certainly wrong.

The Inference Safety Test

A correct SAT inference will always pass ALL three tests:

Test 1
Can I find specific evidence in the passage that makes this conclusion likely? If NO ‚Üí eliminate.
Test 2
Does this inference require assumptions beyond what the passage states? If YES ‚Üí eliminate.
Test 3
Is this the most conservative, text-supported conclusion among the choices? If YES ‚Üí likely correct.

Summary

Key Takeaways

  1. Always ask the Three Key Questions: Big Idea, Structure, Tone.
  2. For main idea questions: predict your answer BEFORE reading the choices.
  3. Eliminate wrong answers that are too narrow, too broad, distorted, or contradicted.
  4. For evidence questions: the evidence must DIRECTLY prove the claim, not just relate to the topic.
  5. SAT inferences are boring, safe, and conservative ‚Äî never exciting leaps.
  6. If you can't point to specific text that supports your answer, eliminate it.
