Why These Matter

The Logic Questions

Transitions and Rhetorical Synthesis are pure logic questions. You don't need to understand the content deeply ‚Äî you need to identify the relationship between ideas and choose the answer that expresses it. These are among the most learnable question types on the SAT.

Transition questions appear in the middle of each R&W module. Rhetorical synthesis questions appear at the very end ‚Äî they're the final questions you'll see. Together, they make up roughly 20% of the section (the "Expression of Ideas" domain).

How to Attack Each Type

Transition Questions
‚ñº

Transition questions give you two sentences and ask you to choose the word or phrase that best connects them. The answer choices are all transition words.

The 3-Step Method

  1. Read both sentences carefully. Ignore the blank at first.
  2. Identify the relationship: Does Sentence 2 continue, contrast, result from, or illustrate Sentence 1?
  3. Choose the transition that matches the relationship. Don't pick a word just because it "sounds right" ‚Äî it must match the logical relationship.
The #1 Mistake: Students pick a transition that sounds good without checking the actual relationship. "However" sounds sophisticated, but if Sentence 2 agrees with Sentence 1, "however" is wrong.

Rhetorical Synthesis Questions
‚ñº

These are the final questions in each R&W module and are among the most challenging. They present bullet-point notes about a topic and ask you to choose a sentence that accomplishes a specific rhetorical goal.

The 4-Step Method

  1. Read the GOAL first. This is the most important step. What specific task must the sentence accomplish? (e.g., "emphasize a difference," "introduce the study's purpose," "highlight a similarity")
  2. Skim the notes to understand the content.
  3. Eliminate any answer that doesn't accomplish the goal, even if it's factually correct based on the notes.
  4. Among remaining choices, pick the most direct and complete accomplishment of the goal.
Key Insight: The notes are often unnecessary! Many rhetorical synthesis questions can be answered from the goal statement alone, since the answer must accomplish that specific purpose regardless of which facts are included.

Common Goals

Emphasize a difference between X and Y
Highlight a similarity between X and Y
Introduce the purpose of a study
Present a finding and its significance
Contrast two researchers' methods
Explain why something matters

Summary

Key Takeaways

  1. Transition questions are logic questions ‚Äî identify the relationship FIRST, then choose the word.
  2. Know the 6 relationships cold: continuation, contrast, cause/effect, example, concession, summary.
  3. For rhetorical synthesis: read the GOAL before anything else. The goal determines the answer.
  4. Eliminate any answer that doesn't accomplish the stated goal, even if it's factually accurate.
  5. These question types are among the most learnable ‚Äî they reward pattern recognition over deep reading.
