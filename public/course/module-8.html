Strategy 1: Process of Elimination (POE)
Princeton Review
‚ñº

Instead of trying to find the right answer, focus on finding and eliminating wrong answers. This is the single most powerful test-taking strategy.

Why POE Works: The Math

Reasons to Eliminate an Answer

  • It contradicts the passage
  • It uses extreme language ("always," "never," "completely") the passage doesn't support
  • It brings in outside information not in the passage
  • It's partially correct but adds something wrong
  • It matches the topic but not the specific question
Use the Bluebook strikethrough feature on every question to physically cross out eliminated answers. Never just "think" an answer is wrong ‚Äî visually remove it.

Strategy 2: Second Half First
Marks Education
‚ñº

Within each R&W module, questions 1‚Äì15 are reading comprehension (harder, more time-consuming) and questions 16‚Äì27 are grammar and conventions (faster, more rule-based). Start with the second half.

Why This Works

  • Grammar questions have definitive right answers based on rules you've memorized. They don't require deep reading.
  • Starting with certainty builds confidence and momentum.
  • You avoid spending too long on a hard reading question early, then rushing grammar questions you would have gotten right.
  • If you run low on time, you're running low on reading questions (where guessing has decent odds) rather than grammar questions (where knowing the rule is nearly guaranteed).
Important: Practice this strategy on at least 2‚Äì3 practice modules before test day. Don't try it for the first time on the real test.

Strategy 3: Personal Order of Difficulty (POOD)
Princeton Review
‚ñº

Not all students find the same questions difficult. Identify which question types are easiest and hardest for you, then tackle questions in your personal order.

How to Build Your POOD

  1. Take a practice test and track your accuracy by question type.
  2. Rank question types from highest accuracy (do first) to lowest (do last).
  3. On test day: quickly scan each question, do your "easy" types first, flag your "hard" types for later.
  4. Use the Mark for Review flag to skip hard questions and come back.

Use the interactive assessment below to build your personal POOD.

Strategy 4: Letter of the Day (LOTD)
Princeton Review
‚ñº

When you must guess, always guess the same letter (e.g., always guess B). This saves time and eliminates "which letter should I pick?" anxiety.

The Rules
  • No penalty for guessing ‚Äî never leave a question blank
  • Pick one letter NOW and commit to it for every guess
  • Random guessing yields the same statistical result regardless of letter
  • A consistent letter saves decision time and mental energy
  • Only use LOTD after you've exhausted POE

Summary

Key Takeaways

  1. POE first: Eliminate wrong answers before choosing. Use strikethrough on every question.
  2. Second Half First: Start with Q16 (grammar) to bank easy points, then do Q1‚ÄìQ15 (reading).
  3. Build your POOD: Know which question types are easiest for YOU and do those first.
  4. LOTD: Pick one letter and always guess that when you're stuck. Never leave blanks.
  5. Master Bluebook: Highlighting, strikethrough, mark for review, and the timer are your best friends.
  6. Practice these strategies on 2‚Äì3 practice modules BEFORE test day.
