Approach

Why Thematic Vocabulary?

Core Principle: Random word lists don't stick. Thematic groups build lasting understanding.

The SAT never tests vocabulary in isolation. Every vocabulary question provides a passage context and asks you to determine meaning from that context. Rather than memorizing random word lists, you'll learn vocabulary organized into the conceptual categories that SAT passages actually use. This builds lasting comprehension because you understand not just individual words, but the intellectual frameworks those words belong to.

Method

How to Study Vocabulary

  1. Introduce 8‚Äì10 words from a single thematic category per session.
  2. For each word: brief definition + SAT-style model sentence + thematic reasoning (why does this word belong here?).
  3. Practice: Short passages with a blank and 4 word choices. All plausible ‚Äî only one fits the specific context.
  4. Discuss wrong answers explicitly. Why is each tempting? What context clue eliminates it?
  5. Review: Quick 5-question context quiz on the previous session's words.
  6. Long-term: 30 minutes daily reading from diverse sources to encounter vocabulary in authentic contexts.
Key Insight

Context Is Everything

The SAT never asks "What does this word mean?" It asks "Which word fits this specific context?"

What the SAT Tests

  • A short passage (3‚Äì5 sentences)
  • A blank where one word is missing
  • Four answers ‚Äî all plausible at first glance
  • Only ONE fits the specific context

What This Means for You

  • Memorizing definitions alone won't help
  • Practice with words IN context sentences
  • Learn to spot context clues
  • Always study WHY wrong answers are wrong
Practice

Sample Question: Words in Context

Practice Question
Marine biologist Dr. Ayana Vasquez has spent two decades studying coral reef ecosystems in the Caribbean. While many researchers have focused on the dramatic decline of reef populations, Vasquez's work takes a more _______ approach, examining not only the damage but also the surprising resilience certain coral species have demonstrated in warming waters.
Which choice completes the text with the most logical and precise word?
Strategy

Spotting Context Clues

These patterns help you eliminate wrong answers and find the right one.

Contrast Clues
while, but, however, although, yet, unlike
"While others focus on decline, she takes a more _______ approach" ‚Üí the blank must contrast with "decline only"
Definition Clues
meaning, that is, in other words, defined as
The passage itself defines or restates the blank's meaning nearby.
Example Clues
such as, for example, including, like
Examples after the blank narrow down what kind of word fits.
Tone Clues
positive/negative/neutral language
Surrounding praise words ‚Üí blank is positive. Surrounding criticism ‚Üí blank is negative.
Strategy

Eliminating Wrong Answers

For vocabulary questions, wrong answers fail in predictable ways.

Wrong Tone
The word has the wrong positive/negative charge. If the passage is praising something, a negative word is wrong even if it relates to the topic.
Wrong Intensity
The word is too strong or too weak. "Revolutionary" when the passage suggests modest change. "Slightly" when describing a dramatic shift.
Wrong Scope
The word relates to the topic but doesn't match the specific meaning. "Provocative" relates to research but doesn't mean multi-layered.
Opposite Meaning
The most obvious trap. "Superficial" is the direct opposite of what's needed. These catch speed-readers.

Summary

Key Takeaways

  1. Vocabulary on the SAT is always about context, never just definitions.
  2. Thematic categories give you frameworks for understanding new words.
  3. Study wrong answers as carefully as right answers.
  4. Context clues (contrast, tone, examples) are your best tools.
  5. Daily challenging reading is the highest-impact long-term habit.
