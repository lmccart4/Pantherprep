Module 5

Geometry — Shapes, Angles, Area & Volume

~15% of the test. No trigonometry — just core geometry you already know, applied to PSAT-style problems.

⏱ ~60 min📋 5 topics + 5 exercises🔨 Phase 2: Build
No Trig on the PSAT 8/9! Unlike the PSAT/NMSQT and SAT, the PSAT 8/9 does NOT test sine, cosine, tangent, or radians. All triangle problems can be solved with the Pythagorean theorem and side ratios. If you see a right triangle, think ratios and a² + b² = c², never SOH-CAH-TOA.
Topic 5A

Lines, Angles & Triangles

5A
Angle Relationships
Vertical, supplementary, complementary, parallel lines + transversal
▸

Basic Angle Pairs

  • Vertical angles: Formed by intersecting lines, across from each other → EQUAL
  • Supplementary angles: On a straight line → add to 180°
  • Complementary angles: Form a right angle → add to 90°

Parallel Lines + Transversal

When a line crosses two parallel lines, it creates 8 angles. You only need to know two values — all angles are either equal or supplementary.

  • Corresponding angles: Same position at each intersection → EQUAL
  • Alternate interior angles: Opposite sides, between the parallels → EQUAL
  • Co-interior (same-side interior): Same side, between the parallels → SUPPLEMENTARY (add to 180°)
Quick Rule: At a parallel lines + transversal crossing, every angle is either x° or (180 − x)°. If you know one angle, you know all eight.
5A+
Triangle Properties
Angle sum, exterior angle theorem, similar triangles, triangle inequality
▸
Angle Sum: Interior angles always add to 180°
Exterior Angle: = sum of the two remote (non-adjacent) interior angles
Similar Triangles: Same shape → proportional sides. Set up a proportion.
Triangle Inequality: Any side < sum of the other two sides

Example: A triangle has angles 40° and 65°. The third angle = 180° − 40° − 65° = 75°.

Exterior angle example: If two interior angles are 50° and 70°, the exterior angle at the third vertex = 50° + 70° = 120°.

Similar triangles shortcut: If two triangles share an angle AND have parallel sides, they're similar. Set up proportions: short/short = long/long = hyp/hyp.
Topic 5B

Pythagorean Theorem & Special Right Triangles

5B
Pythagorean Theorem & Common Triples
a² + b² = c² and the triples you should memorize
▸
Pythagorean Theorem: a² + b² = c²
c = hypotenuse (longest side, opposite the right angle). a and b = legs.

Example: Legs are 6 and 8. Hypotenuse = √(36 + 64) = √100 = 10.

Recognizing triples saves time — you don't need to calculate!

Common Pythagorean Triples (memorize these!):

3 – 4 – 5
5 – 12 – 13
8 – 15 – 17
7 – 24 – 25

Multiples work too! 6-8-10 and 9-12-15 are both from the 3-4-5 family.

PSAT Trap: Don't assume c is the longest number you see — c must be the hypotenuse (opposite the right angle). If the problem asks for a leg, rearrange: a² = c² − b².
5B+
Special Right Triangles (by Side Ratios)
30-60-90 and 45-45-90 — no trig needed
▸

These triangles appear on nearly every PSAT. Learn the side ratios and you can find any missing side.

30-60-90
1 : √3 : 2
Short leg : Long leg : Hypotenuse
(Opp 30° : Opp 60° : Opp 90°)
45-45-90
1 : 1 : √2
Leg : Leg : Hypotenuse
(Two legs always equal)

How to Use Ratios

30-60-90 example: If the short leg (opposite 30°) is 5, then long leg = 5√3 ≈ 8.66, hypotenuse = 10.

45-45-90 example: If each leg is 7, then hypotenuse = 7√2 ≈ 9.90.

Working backwards: If the hypotenuse of a 30-60-90 is 12, the short leg = 12/2 = 6, long leg = 6√3.

No Trig Needed: On the PSAT 8/9, every "find the missing side" right triangle problem can be solved with either the Pythagorean theorem or these ratios. You never need sin/cos/tan.

5C
Key Patterns & Composite Shapes
The ⅓ pattern, shaded regions, and hidden triangles
▸

The ⅓ Pattern

Cones and pyramids are always ⅓ of the "full" version. Cone = ⅓ × cylinder. Pyramid = ⅓ × prism. This is the easiest way to remember them.

Composite Shapes (Shaded Regions)

The PSAT loves shaded region problems. The strategy is always:

Shaded Area = Area of OUTER shape − Area of INNER shape

Example: A circle with radius 5 is inscribed in a square. Shaded area = square − circle = (10)² − π(5)² = 100 − 25π.

Hidden Right Triangles

Many PSAT problems contain a hidden right triangle. Look for:

  • Diagonals of rectangles → creates right triangles
  • Heights dropped inside triangles → creates two right triangles
  • Distances between two points → horizontal + vertical = legs of a right triangle
Common trap: Height ≠ slant side! The height of a triangle is ALWAYS perpendicular to the base. If they give a slant height, you may need Pythagorean theorem to find the actual height first.

5D
Circle Equations, Arcs & Angles
Reading center-radius form, arc/sector fractions, central vs. inscribed angles
▸

Reading the Circle Equation

(x − 3)² + (y + 2)² = 25 → Center = (3, −2), Radius = 5 (because √25 = 5)

Watch the signs: (x − h) means center x = +h. (y + 2) means (y − (−2)), so center y = −2.

Arc Length & Sector Area

Both use the same fraction: central angle / 360. Then multiply by the full circumference (arc length) or full area (sector area).

Example: A circle with radius 6 has a 90° sector.

Arc length = (90/360) × 2π(6) = ¼ × 12π = 3π

Sector area = (90/360) × π(6)² = ¼ × 36π = 9π

Angle Rules

  • Central angle (vertex at center) = intercepted arc
  • Inscribed angle (vertex on circle) = HALF the intercepted arc
  • Tangent line: Touches circle at one point and is perpendicular to the radius (90°)
Desmos Power Move: Type (x−2)²+(y+3)²=25 directly to graph any circle. Click intersection points with lines.
Topic 5E

Coordinate Geometry

5E
Distance & Midpoint Formulas
Finding lengths and midpoints on the coordinate plane
▸
Distance: d = √[(x₂−x₁)² + (y₂−y₁)²]
This IS the Pythagorean theorem in disguise!
Midpoint: M = ((x₁+x₂)/2, (y₁+y₂)/2)
Average the x's and average the y's

Example: Distance from (1, 2) to (4, 6) = √[(4−1)² + (6−2)²] = √[9 + 16] = √25 = 5.

That's a 3-4-5 right triangle! Horizontal distance = 3, vertical distance = 4.

Don't memorize the distance formula separately. Just draw a right triangle on the coordinate plane. The horizontal and vertical distances are the legs. Use a² + b² = c².
Math Trap Taxonomy

Four Traps in Geometry

These traps catch students who rush through geometry problems. Know them and you'll dodge easy-to-lose points.

1
🎯 The Wrong Target
Found the value of x but the question asked for 2x, the perimeter, or the area. Always re-read what they're actually asking.
Found leg = 5, but they asked for hypotenuse. Answer: √(25+144) = 13, not 5
2
📐 The Phantom Height
Used the slant side as the height. Height must ALWAYS be perpendicular to the base — if they give a slant, you need Pythagorean theorem first.
Triangle: base=10, slant=13, actual height=12 (from 5-12-13 triple). A=½(10)(12)=60, NOT 65
3
🔄 The Sign Flip
Misread the signs in circle equations. (x+4) means center x = −4, not +4. The signs in (x−h)²+(y−k)²=r² are opposite to what you see.
(x+3)²+(y−2)²=49 → Center (−3, 2), NOT (3, −2). Radius = 7
4
📏 The Unit Mismatch
Mixed inches and feet, or cm and m, without converting. All measurements must be in the same unit before computing area or volume.
Rectangle: 2 ft by 18 in. Convert first: 2ft = 24in. A = 24×18 = 432 sq in, NOT 36
